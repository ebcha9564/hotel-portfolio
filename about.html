WHERE THE TRADITION BEGINS IN KOREA

Traditional Korean house that reflects the architectural wisdom,
aesthetics, and lifestyle of the Korean people Originating during
the Joseon Dynasty (1392-1897), the hanok is designed
to harmonize with nature, climate, and seasons Unlike Western-style
buildings that emphasize vertical structures and enclosed spaces,
hanok architecture focuses on horizontal lines, open courtyards,
and seamless integration with the surrounding environment

HISTORIC CULTURE AND ART COMBINES

Today, hanok are treasured as cultural heritage and symbols
of Korean identity. Many historical hanok villages,
such as Bukchon Hanok Village in Seoul and
Jeonju Hanok Village, have been preserved or restored, attracting both
locals and tourists. Some hanok have been converted into
guesthouses, cafes, galleries, or cultural centers, allowing people to
experience traditional Korean architecture in modern contexts

CONVENTIONAL DESIGN AND STRUCTURED HOTEL

One of the defining characteristics of hanok is its use of natural
materials. Wood, clay, stone, straw, and paper are
the main components, chosen not only for their availability
but also for their ability to breathe and adjust
to changing weather. The structure is typically built
on a raised stone platform to protect it from moisture.

At the core of hanok architecture lies pungsu-jiri (풍수지리), Korea’s version of feng
shui. This traditional geomantic principle emphasizes selecting a site and orienting
a building to achieve harmony with natural energy (or gi). A hanok is typically positioned
with a mountain behind it for protection and a river or open space in front for prosperity
. The layout, orientation, and proportions of the house are designed to balance yin and yang
energies and create a sense of peace and well-being for its inhabitants.
